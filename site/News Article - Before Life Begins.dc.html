The LADA Vision

Before Life Begins

What if the true measure of reproductive medicine is not only the pregnancies it achieves, but the people it prepares?

A new standard of care — beginning before treatment, continuing beyond it.

For almost twenty years, the LADA Reproductive Medicine Center in Odesa has accompanied families through one of the most demanding experiences in adult life. Over that time, and across more than 3,500 families, a single question began to reshape how its founder understood her own profession: are we measuring the right thing?

Reproductive medicine has long defined its progress through a sequence of clinical milestones — a consultation, a diagnosis, an embryo, a pregnancy, a birth. Each is essential. Yet none, on its own, describes what actually happens to the person moving through it. That observation is the starting point of the LADA Vision, the framework Svitlana Yashchenok brought to the World Woman Forum in Davos this summer.

"The people we care for do not simply pass through procedures," she says. "They change. They arrive uncertain and often afraid, and — whatever the medical outcome — they can leave more informed, more resilient and better prepared for parenthood." That transformation, she has come to believe, is not a side effect of good care. It is the purpose of it.

"Preparation does not begin at treatment. It begins long before life itself begins."

The reasoning is practical as much as humane. In controlled studies of women undergoing IVF, those who received structured psychological support achieved markedly higher pregnancy rates than those who received standard care alone — in one widely cited study, a rise from 20 to 55 percent. The same patients, the same medicine, a different standard of care. Yashchenok is careful to note that effect sizes vary across trials and populations, and that no single study settles a question of this kind. But the direction of the evidence, she argues, is consistent, and its implication is clear: emotional wellbeing belongs inside the clinical pathway, not beside it.

The mechanisms are increasingly well understood. Chronic stress affects the neuroendocrine systems that govern reproduction. Supported patients are more likely to complete treatment cycles, to follow protocols precisely, and to make decisions calmly rather than under duress. Care for the mind and care for the body, in this reading, are not separate projects.

Five Commitments, One Method

The LADA difference, Yashchenok is quick to clarify, is not a single technique. It is the disciplined combination of five commitments — a broader definition of care. Science remains the bedrock: rigorous diagnostics, laboratory excellence and evidence-based protocols. Education turns patients into calm, confident participants in their own treatment. Prevention addresses health early, widening what medicine can later achieve. Emotional wellbeing is treated as clinical work, not comfort added at the margins. And conscious preparation readies the whole person — physically, emotionally and relationally — for the role ahead.

None of these commitments replaces medical excellence. Each extends it — from the biology of conception to the readiness of the people at its centre. Across thousands of journeys, the center observed the same four shifts recur: from helplessness to responsibility, from fear to understanding, from isolation to partnership, and from wanting a baby to becoming parents.

"The patients who did best were not only treated well. They were prepared well."

If preparation and transformation matter, then success must be measured by more than a single rate. Pregnancy rates remain the most important clinical indicator, and always will be. But a fuller definition, Yashchenok proposes, includes healthy children born to prepared parents; confident parents, informed and steady; resilient families, strengthened by the journey rather than strained by it; and better decisions, made consciously rather than under duress. Taken together, these point toward a single aim — healthier generations.

This is not a model designed in theory, she is at pains to point out. It was arrived at in practice — case by case, year after year. The patients who did best, she noticed, were rarely those with the simplest diagnoses. They were the ones who understood their situation, who felt supported, and who approached treatment as active participants. Over time, that observation hardened into a method, and the method into a vision.

No single clinic can deliver this vision alone. To change the standard of care, Yashchenok argues, the model must be codified, taught, certified and studied — embedded in the systems through which societies deliver reproductive health. Medical centres, education, certification, research and government partnerships form one connected ecosystem, each element reinforcing the others.

What if success begins before pregnancy?

The future of reproductive medicine will not be defined only by better laboratories, better genetics or better technology, remarkable as those advances are. It will be defined, in Yashchenok's telling, by how well we prepare the people who will create the next generation. That preparation — physical, emotional, relational and intellectual — is work that medicine has too often left to chance.

A healthier family contributes, in a direct and measurable way, to a healthier society. If we can prepare people as carefully as we treat them, the result will not only be more pregnancies. It will be healthier generations — the outcome that, in the end, has always mattered most.
