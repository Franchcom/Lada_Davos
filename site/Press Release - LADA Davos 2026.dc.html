LADA Calls for a Broader Measure of Success in Reproductive Medicine

At the World Woman Forum in Davos, the Ukrainian fertility center presents a framework that treats education, prevention and emotional wellbeing as clinical work — citing research in which structured psychological support raised IVF pregnancy rates from 20 to 55 percent.

DAVOS, Switzerland, 9 July 2026 — Reproductive medicine should be judged by more than pregnancy rates, the founder of a Ukrainian fertility center told delegates at the World Woman Forum on Wednesday, calling for the preparation of the whole person — physically, emotionally and intellectually — to be treated as clinical work in its own right.

In a keynote address titled Before Life Begins, Svitlana Yashchenok, founder of the LADA Reproductive Medicine Center in Odesa, set out a model that begins before treatment and continues beyond it. The approach, she said, does not replace medical excellence but extends it, adding education, prevention, emotional support and conscious preparation to the standard clinical pathway.

Yashchenok pointed to published research in which women undergoing in-vitro fertilisation with structured psychological support achieved higher pregnancy rates than those receiving standard care alone — a rise from 20 to 55 percent in one widely cited study. She cautioned that effect sizes vary across trials and populations, but said the direction of the evidence was consistent.

The center, which has accompanied more than 3,500 families over nearly two decades, argues that clinical milestones — a diagnosis, an embryo, a pregnancy, a birth — describe moments rather than people, and that two patients reaching the same medical endpoint may have lived entirely different journeys.

"A milestone marks a moment; it does not describe a person. Preparation does not begin at treatment. It begins long before life itself begins."

— Svitlana Yashchenok, Founder, LADA Reproductive Medicine Center

She said the model reframes the goal of care from a single medical result to a broader set of outcomes: healthy children, confident parents, resilient families and better-informed decisions.

"The patients who did best were not only treated well. They were prepared well. A healthy embryo is only part of the outcome we should be aiming for."

— Svitlana Yashchenok

Yashchenok said the approach could not be delivered by any single clinic and called for it to be codified, taught, certified and studied through partnerships with governments, universities, hospitals and research institutions. LADA is seeking collaborators rather than clients, she said.

"If we can prepare people as carefully as we treat them, the result will not only be more pregnancies. It will be healthier generations."

— Svitlana Yashchenok

The World Woman Forum, held in Davos from 9 to 17 July 2026, convenes leaders across health, policy, research and industry. LADA's framework, presented there as a vision paper, is offered as a foundation for collaboration rather than a commercial product.

About LADA

The LADA Reproductive Medicine Center, based in Odesa, Ukraine, has provided fertility care to more than 3,500 families over nearly twenty years. LADA integrates science, education, prevention, emotional wellbeing and conscious preparation into a single connected model of reproductive care, with the aim of supporting individuals before, during and beyond treatment.

About Svitlana Yashchenok

Svitlana Yashchenok is the founder of the LADA Reproductive Medicine Center. Over more than eighteen years and 3,500 families, she developed an approach to fertility care that treats the preparation and wellbeing of the whole person as central to clinical outcomes. She is the author of the LADA Vision and a speaker at the World Woman Forum 2026.
