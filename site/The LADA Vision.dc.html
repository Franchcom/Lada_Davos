Executive Summary 01 / 12

Are we measuring
the right thing?

For almost twenty years, the LADA Reproductive Medicine Center has accompanied families through one of the most demanding experiences in adult life. Over that time, a single question has reshaped how we understand our work.

Reproductive medicine has long defined its progress through a sequence of clinical milestones — a consultation, a diagnosis, an embryo, a pregnancy, a birth. Each is essential. Yet none, on its own, describes what actually happens to the person moving through it.

The people we care for do not simply pass through procedures. They change. They arrive uncertain and often afraid, and — whatever the medical outcome — they can leave more informed, more resilient and better prepared for parenthood. That transformation, we have come to believe, is not a side effect of good care. It is the purpose of it.

This paper sets out a framework we call the LADA Vision: an approach that treats preparation of the whole person as clinical work in its own right — beginning before treatment and continuing well beyond it. The reasoning is practical as much as humane. In published research, adding structured psychological support to standard IVF raised pregnancy rates from 20 to 55 percent: the same patients, the same medicine, a different standard of care.

What follows is not the description of a clinic. It is a proposal for how reproductive medicine might evolve — and an invitation to those who would help build it.

The Global Challenge 02 / 12

Milestones remain essential. They are no longer sufficient.

The milestone model has served reproductive medicine well. Its limits are becoming difficult to ignore.

For decades the field has organised itself around a clear and clinically sound sequence of events. Diagnosis identifies the obstacle. Treatment addresses it. A pregnancy confirms success; a birth completes it. This structure has produced extraordinary advances, and nothing in the LADA Vision asks the profession to abandon it.

But a milestone marks a moment; it does not describe a person. Two patients can reach the same clinical endpoint having lived entirely different journeys — one supported, informed and steady; the other frightened, isolated and depleted. The medical record shows no difference between them. The human reality could not be more different.

There is also a question of timing. By the time many patients reach a fertility clinic, the factors that most influence their outcome — physical health, emotional readiness, understanding, lifestyle — have been forming for years. Medicine that begins only at the point of treatment inherits conditions it did little to shape.

Preparation does not begin at treatment. It begins long before life itself begins.

The opportunity, then, is not to replace the milestones but to widen the frame around them — to begin earlier, to prepare more completely, and to measure more of what matters.

Why This Matters 03 / 12

A challenge that is demographic, emotional and societal.

The case for change reaches well beyond any single clinic — and beyond any single family.

I
Demographic
Across much of the world, birth rates are declining and the age of first parenthood is rising. More families are turning to assisted reproduction, often later and under greater pressure. Demand is growing precisely as the margins for success grow narrower.
II
Emotional
The emotional weight of infertility is well documented; its psychological burden has been compared to that of serious chronic illness. Stress, anxiety and isolation are not peripheral to the clinical picture — they influence adherence, decision-making and, the evidence suggests, outcomes themselves.
III
Societal
The health, stability and preparedness of new parents shape the earliest environment of the next generation. A healthier, better-prepared family contributes, in a direct and measurable way, to a healthier society.

Reproductive medicine cannot resolve these forces alone. But it can evolve to meet them — by treating preparation, understanding and emotional wellbeing as core clinical concerns rather than optional extras.

The LADA Difference 04 / 12

Five commitments that define our method.

The LADA difference is not a single technique. It is the disciplined combination of five commitments — a broader definition of care.

01
Science
Rigorous diagnostics, laboratory excellence and evidence-based protocols remain the bedrock of every outcome. The LADA Vision extends medical science; it never substitutes for it.
02
Education
Patients who understand the biology, the probabilities and the choices before them make calmer, more confident decisions — and become genuine participants in their own care.
03
Prevention
Reproductive health is shaped years before a consultation. Addressing lifestyle, metabolic and environmental factors early widens what medicine can later achieve.
04
Emotional Wellbeing
Fertility treatment is among the most stressful experiences in adult life. Structured psychological support is not a comfort added at the margins; it belongs within the clinical pathway.
05
Conscious Preparation
Parenthood begins before conception. Preparing the whole person — physically, emotionally and relationally — improves both the likelihood and the quality of the outcome.

None of these commitments replaces medical excellence. Each extends it — from the biology of conception to the readiness of the people at its centre.

Scientific Foundation 05 / 12

Care for the whole person is not sentiment. It is evidence.

The integration of psychological care into fertility treatment is supported by published research — and its most striking finding is also the simplest to state.

In controlled studies of women undergoing IVF, those who received structured psychological support achieved markedly higher pregnancy rates than those who received standard care alone.

Standard IVF + Psychological Support
20% → 55%
Pregnancy rate with structured psychological support, compared with standard care — the same patients, the same medicine.
Domar et al., Fertility & Sterility (2000) · Zhou et al., Frontiers in Psychology (2021)

The mechanisms are plausible and increasingly well understood. Chronic stress affects the neuroendocrine systems that govern reproduction. Supported patients are more likely to complete treatment cycles, to follow protocols precisely, and to make decisions calmly rather than under duress. Care for the mind and care for the body are not separate projects.

These findings should be read with appropriate caution: effect sizes vary across studies and populations, and no single trial settles a question of this kind. But the direction of the evidence is consistent, and its practical implication is clear. Emotional wellbeing belongs inside the clinical pathway — not beside it.

The Human Transformation Model 06 / 12

Four shifts that happen before, during and beyond treatment.

Across thousands of journeys we have observed the same four shifts recur. We call them the LADA Human Transformation Model.

Helplessness→Responsibility
Patients move from experiencing treatment as something done to them toward actively shaping their reproductive future.
Fear→Understanding
As knowledge grows, the uncertainty patients arrive with gives way to informed calm.
Isolation→Partnership
The journey stops being carried alone; patients, partners and clinicians become a single team.
Wanting a baby→Becoming parents
The goal widens from a medical result to the lifelong work of parenthood — which begins, in our experience, well before birth.

These transformations are not the pleasant by-product of a good outcome. They can occur regardless of the medical result — and when they do, people leave stronger, wiser and more hopeful than they arrived. That, too, is a form of success.

Redefining Success 07 / 12

Success, redefined.

If preparation and transformation matter, then success must be measured by more than a single rate.

Pregnancy rates remain the most important clinical indicator, and they always will be. But they describe only one dimension of what reproductive medicine can achieve. A fuller definition looks like this.

Healthy Children— born to prepared, well-supported parents.
Confident Parents— informed, steady and ready for the role.
Resilient Families— strengthened by the journey rather than strained by it.
Better Decisions— made consciously, not under duress.
Healthier Generations

A clinic that optimises only for pregnancy will make different choices than one that also cares whether the resulting family is prepared, informed and well. The LADA Vision chooses the wider aim.

The LADA Ecosystem 08 / 12

One connected ecosystem.

No single clinic can deliver this vision alone. To change the standard of care, the model must be codified, taught, certified and studied.

The transformation described in these pages cannot remain the practice of one centre. It must be embedded in the systems through which societies deliver reproductive health. Five elements form that ecosystem.

Medical Centres
Where the integrated model is practised and refined.
Education
Training clinicians and informing patients at scale.
Certification
Protecting the integrity of the method as it spreads.
Research
Extending the evidence base that underpins the model.
Government Partnerships
Embedding the approach within public health systems.
Together: strategic healthcare innovation.

Each element reinforces the others. Research refines the model; education transmits it; certification safeguards it; partnerships bring it to scale. Together they form a single, connected system for advancing reproductive health.

About the Author 10 / 12

A conclusion reached in practice, over two decades.

The LADA Vision was not designed in theory. It was arrived at in practice — case by case, year after year.

Svitlana Yashchenok founded the LADA Reproductive Medicine Center in Odesa, Ukraine, and has since accompanied more than 3,500 families through the reproductive journey. It was in that work that the limits of the milestone model became impossible to overlook.

She observed that the patients who did best were rarely those with the simplest diagnoses. They were the ones who understood their situation, who felt supported, and who approached treatment as active participants rather than passive recipients. Over time, that observation hardened into a method — and the method into a vision.

Her contribution is less that of a clinician than of a systems thinker: someone willing to question the profession's own measures of success, and to propose a more complete one. This paper is the articulation of that thinking.

Conclusion 11 / 12
What if success begins before pregnancy?

The future of reproductive medicine will not be defined only by better laboratories, better genetics or better technology — remarkable as those advances are. It will be defined by how well we prepare the people who will create the next generation.

That preparation — physical, emotional, relational and intellectual — is work that medicine has too often left to chance. The LADA Vision proposes that we take it seriously, begin it early, and measure it honestly.

A healthier family contributes to a healthier society. If we can prepare people as carefully as we treat them, the result will not only be more pregnancies. It will be healthier generations — the outcome that has always mattered most.

LADA is now working to build this model beyond a single clinic — and is inviting partners to help establish it as a standard of care.

We are not presenting a completed model. We are presenting a future that we invite others to help build.
